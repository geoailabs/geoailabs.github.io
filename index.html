Applied AI for cities, climate, and infrastructure

Building geospatial AI that helps institutions plan better, respond faster, and allocate resources more intelligently.

Led by Anupam Sobti, the lab’s public research agenda centers on sustainable communities through geospatial computer vision, applied machine learning, and deployable systems.

GeoAI Lab operates at the boundary of research and deployment. We work on urban mobility, climate adaptation, water systems, agriculture, and accessibility, with a bias toward tools that decision-makers can actually use.

Focus Areas

Program lines that speak to both fundable impact and ambitious technical work.

Urban mobility

Transit and movement models for fast-growing cities

Planning support for transit systems, underserved routes, and mobility interventions where public infrastructure has to stretch further.

Urban climate

Climate intelligence that maps risk at neighborhood scale

Spatial models for heat, flooding, and climate adaptation, geared toward decisions that can be justified to governments and funders.

Urban accessibility

Tools that make place legible to people who are usually ignored

Work on accessibility mapping, public engagement, and participatory workflows that turn lived experience into operational evidence.

Urban water

Water metrics, flood assessment, and decision support

Modeling and monitoring systems that connect hydrology, infrastructure, and accountability in vulnerable regions.

Agriculture

Multimodal foundation models for crop and land intelligence

Research on crop representation learning, time series, and world models for agricultural planning and resilience.

Why This Lab

Structured to be credible to funders and magnetic to researchers.

For funders and partners

  • Clear sector alignment across resilience, inclusion, food, and infrastructure.
  • Projects can be framed as pilots, data infrastructure, or longer-horizon research programs.
  • Partnership model spans public agencies, nonprofits, universities, and technical organizations.
  • Lab themes are broad enough for portfolio growth but concrete enough for measurable outcomes.

For research and engineering staff

  • Problems are consequential, spatially grounded, and technically non-trivial.
  • Work sits at the interface of ML systems, domain science, geospatial data, and deployment.
  • The agenda supports both publications and production-grade prototypes.
  • Researchers can shape new programs rather than fitting into a narrow pre-defined lane.
